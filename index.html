Is the allocation of seats of Geographical Constituencies in the LegCo resonable?



Since the election system for the Hong Kong Legislative Council has been around for a long time, the division of the five major constituencies in Hong Kong Island, Kowloon East, Kowloon West, New Territories East and New Territories West has been in place, but the number of constituency seats has been changing.

Theoretically, the Electoral Affairs Commission, in accordance with the Legislative Council Ordinance and the Electoral Affairs Commission Ordinance, determines the allocation of seats and the allocation of seats with reference to the demographic figures of the districts, the uniqueness of the community, the local links and the natural characteristics of the constituencies. However, in the actual operation, even if the EAC has always stressed that "political factors are not considered", the delimitation and allocation of seats will still cause different disputes. For example, in 2014, it was reported that the Government intends to change the existing five constituencies to nine in the 2016 election and three to five seats for each constituency. This raises the question of different people in society, and many believe that it will be detrimental to small and medium-sized political parties and radicals.

In view of the significant impact of the demarcation and allocation of seats on the results of the election, we have analyzed the electoral arrangements for the last five on the basis of the public information of the Registration and Electoral Office, taking into account the estimated population, number of voters and the number of seats.




During the last five Legislative Council elections, the population of Hong Kong increased from nearly 6.74 million in 2000 to nearly 7.37 million in 2016, and the number of registered voters increased from nearly 2.06 million to nearly 3.78 million. As of the Legislative Council election in 2016, the voter registration rate was about 51.27%, that is, slightly more than half of the population was eligible to vote in the geographical constituency elections.

On the other hand, with the change of the political system, the number of seats in the geographical constituency also increased from 24 seats in 2000 to 35 seats in 2016. If we divide the total number of registered voters by the total number of constituency seats, we can broadly reflect the number of voters that each member can effectively represent. And the number of electors representing the average number of seats on each delegate dropped from 280,704 in 2000 to 210585 in 2016, due to the increase in the number of seats from 2000 to 2016, About 25%.

How many seats should be allocated in each district?

In addition to analyzing the representativeness of seats in each constituency, we can also analyze whether the number of seats allocated per district is reasonable from the actual population size. At present, the EAC divides seats according to the population ratio. As the proportion of the population in each district is almost impossible to be an integer, the EAC has determined that the number of seats in the district is similar to the population.

The number of "income" refers to the number of seats in the geographical constituency, which is simply the number of seats represented by the number of seats in the district. The percentage of income is the percentage of the difference between the estimated number of people and the number of income. In simple terms, if it is a positive number, it means that the population is expected to be larger than the income. That is, the constituency should get more seats, and vice versa.




We according to this algorithm analysis of the last five constituency seats to see what changes. The results show that the constituencies have their own unique trajectory. The number of income in the New Territories West is about 7% of the long-term deviation, which reflects that they should have more seats. In contrast, the number of Hong Kong Island districts in 2000 to 2012 The percentage has been less than -4%, reflecting that it should have received fewer seats, and then after 2016 was subtracted from the seats, the number of deviation from the percentage will return to almost zero the perfect ratio; and Kowloon West is showing a more volatile State, which is the number of its population and the number of seats in the calendar year to a greater change.

So why is the Government not adopting a more accurate seat allocation scheme? Originally, according to the existing Legislative Council Ordinance (19), the number of Members to be elected in each geographical constituency should not be less than five and no more than nine. Therefore, the addition of a seat in the New Territories West will exceed the existing law.

Of course, this provision is not static, and the law has been amended in 2011 to reflect that there is still room for improvement in the allocation of seats in the district. In fact, the EAC has also taken into account some proposals that can be made without amendment, such as the inclusion of certain areas of the New Territories West constituency in other constituencies, but in the end they are due to the imposition of a judgment.

It is expected that the population, the number of voters and the number of seats will be interrelated and will have a certain effect on the representativeness and fairness of the elections. Therefore, the subject should be further considered in the future.
